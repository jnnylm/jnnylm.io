# Mini Project 5

## Hypothesis: 
- Professor Wirfs-Brock's movement pattern shows a statistically significant shift from long-distance travel during her travel days between Denver, Seattle, and Walla Walla, to low-distance, highly localized movement once she is in Walla Walla on March 10.

---

### Visualization 1

Distance from Olin Hall Over Time (Global Movement)
- This visualization shows Prof. Wirfs-Brock’s distance from Olin Hall over time and captures her full travel trajectory across multiple locations. The pattern forms a clear U-shaped curve, reflecting long-distance travel from Denver to Seattle, then to Walla Walla, followed by a return trip back through Seattle to Denver. A noticeable gap of approximately 340 km appears between the Seattle and Walla Walla segments, which likely corresponds to a brief stop at Seattle-Tacoma International Airport, since there are no direct flights between Denver and Walla Walla. Sharp increases in distance indicate major transit events, while flatter segments represent periods of local movement or stationary behavior.

### Visualization 2

Distance from Olin Hall (Walla Walla Only Zoom-In)
- This visualization isolates the period when Prof. Wirfs-Brock is in Walla Walla and focuses on local-scale movement. Unlike the global view, distances remain tightly constrained between approximately 0 and 2 kilometers. This sharp reduction in range indicates a transition from long-distance travel to localized movement within a small geographic area. The low variability in distance values suggests consistent activity within Walla Walla rather than movement between distant locations. This supports the hypothesis that once in Walla Walla, movement becomes highly localized.

### Visualization 3

Mapbox Visualization of Movement in Walla Walla
- This Mapbox visualization displays Prof. Wirfs-Brock’s movement within Walla Walla using geographic coordinates. There are no large spatial jumps, and movement remains confined to short distances within the city. ## Conclusion

Across all three visualizations, a consistent pattern emerges that supports the hypothesis. Prof. Wirfs-Brock’s movement clearly shifts from large-scale, intercity travel to localized movement once she arrives in Walla Walla on March 10. The global distance-over-time plot shows distinct phases of travel between Denver, Seattle, and Walla Walla, with sharp transitions indicating long-distance movement. In contrast, the zoomed-in Walla Walla view shows a dramatic reduction in spatial range, with all movement confined within approximately 0–2 kilometers. The Mapbox visualization further reinforces this finding by showing a tightly clustered movement pattern with no large geographic jumps. Together, these results provide strong evidence of a behavioral shift from transit-based mobility to localized activity, demonstrating a clear change in movement patterns before and after arrival in Walla Walla.
